In class today, we went over an intro to accessible design, with a focus on visually accessible design for the web. Three main take aways were:

  • Disabilities vary, and their severity ranges across a spectrum. Designers should keep this in mind, ensuring spaces/objects/applications address the needs of many users
  • There are a variety of tools and resources online, both for learning about how to design with accessibility in mind, and to assess if you web applications meet certain accessibility requirements
  • The four principles of accessible design (perceptibility, operability, simplicity, and forgiveness) provide a solid basis for creating spaces/objects/applications that meet the needs of many, not just a few. Aside from ensuring designs are accessible, they also help ensure your design is fundamentally better

In class today, we went over the linear story-telling process, focusing on the process of effective story-boarding. Three main take aways were:

  • How important having a comprehensive written script is early in the pre-production stages. I knew that scripts were often written early on; however, I didn’t completely realize they needed to include such detailed information regarding camera shots, descriptions of the environment, character movements, etc. The amount of “visualization” that happens in a written, script-bases sense before any storyboarding panels are even written was surprising to me
  • How much contextual information is necessary and useful in storyboarding panels. I had assumed that most storyboards were simply examples of hand drawn “stills” from the movie, and that any contextual information involving camera movements, dialogue, or emotions/expressions was either written as accompanying text, left separate in the script, and verbally communicated during pitches. However, I now know how much information about everything happening within a shot is shown in a series of storyboarding panels, and even individual panels
  • That seemingly simple, short scenes still warrant proper explanations via storyboarding panels, which may mean multiple panels and/or additional contextual information. A five second scene may need five panels if the action taking place warrants the additional clarity

In class today, we had a joint liner media and non-linear media workshop covering the basics of sound design. Aside from how nice it was to have a nearly combined class again, the three main take aways from the workshop were:

  • How much proper and thought out sound design can improve animated media. The added life and feeling that sound effects, atmospheric sound, and in some cases, music, can add to a scene are very pronounced. Even the most beautiful animation can fall flat if the sound design isn't considered
  • The forethought that needs to go into setting up a quality narration session. Aside from the person you choose as narrator, which in itself is a major factor, careful consideration needs to be given when setting up the recording space, how you communicate with the narrator/voice actor, and how you arrange the materials you give them for reference and reading
  • That quality sound effects don't need to be elaborate or expensive. Often times, we can record sounds from our day to day lives, from experimenting with tools and resources we have, or from free online libraries. These sound effects can be just as effective for our purposes, with far less hassle and associated costs
